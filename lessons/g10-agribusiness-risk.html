Managing Risk in Agribusiness

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Sources of Risk Importance of Risk Management Risk Management Strategies

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the sources of risk and uncertainty in farming businesses
  • Explain why risk management is important in agribusiness
  • Describe strategies used to manage and reduce agricultural risk

What is Risk in Agribusiness?

In any business, risk is the possibility that things will not go as planned and that money will be lost. Agribusiness faces particularly high levels of risk because farmers depend on weather, living organisms, and unpredictable market prices — all of which are largely outside their control.

Sources of Risk in Agribusiness

  • Production risk — the risk that crops or animals will fail to produce expected yields due to weather (drought, flood, frost), pests, disease, or poor management. This is the most common risk in PNG agriculture.
  • Price/market risk — the risk that prices will fall after the farmer has already planted and invested in a crop. Global commodity prices for coffee and cocoa can drop significantly.
  • Financial risk — the risk of not being able to repay loans or cover operating costs if income falls short. Many smallholder farmers in PNG have limited access to credit.
  • Human risk — illness, death, or departure of key people in the business can disrupt production and management
  • Institutional risk — changes in government policy, land tenure disputes, or unreliable infrastructure (roads, electricity) that affect the business
  • Environmental/climate risk — increasingly severe weather events, sea level rise, and changing rainfall patterns due to climate change

Why Risk Management is Important

Without managing risk, agribusinesses can:

  • Lose their entire investment in a single bad season
  • Fail to repay loans and become indebted
  • Be forced to abandon farming permanently
  • Leave families without food or income

Effective risk management does not eliminate risk — it reduces the impact of bad outcomes so the business can survive and recover.

Risk Management Strategies

  • Diversification — growing multiple crops or combining crops with livestock so that if one enterprise fails, others can compensate. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
  • Savings — maintaining financial reserves to cover costs during bad seasons
  • Crop insurance — paying a premium in good years so that losses are partially covered in bad years (limited availability in PNG)
  • Improved crop varieties — using drought-tolerant, disease-resistant varieties that reduce production risk
  • Irrigation — reducing dependence on unreliable rainfall
  • Contract farming — agreeing in advance on a buyer and price for a crop, reducing market risk
  • Market information — monitoring market prices and selling when prices are favourable rather than immediately after harvest (when many farmers sell and prices are lowest)
  • Cooperatives — joining a farmer group or cooperative gives individual farmers more bargaining power with buyers and access to shared resources
🇵🇬 PNG Example: Coffee farmers in the Highlands face significant price risk because global coffee prices are set in New York and London. Joining a cooperative and obtaining Fair Trade or organic certification are strategies that can secure better, more stable prices.

Key Terms

RiskThe possibility that actual outcomes will differ from planned outcomes, potentially resulting in financial loss
DiversificationSpreading production across multiple crops or enterprises so that failure of one does not destroy the whole business
Contract farmingAn arrangement where a buyer agrees in advance to purchase a farmer's crop at a set price, reducing market risk
CooperativeAn organisation owned and operated by a group of farmers who work together to buy inputs, process products, or access markets
Production riskThe risk that crops or animals will fail to produce expected yields due to weather, pests, disease, or poor management

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Risk in agribusiness includes production, price, financial, human, institutional, and environmental risk
  • Production risk (crop failure from weather or pests) is the most common risk in Papua New Guinea
  • Risk management does not eliminate risk — it reduces the impact so the business can survive
  • Key strategies include diversification, savings, improved varieties, irrigation, contract farming, and cooperatives
  • Joining a cooperative can help individual smallholder farmers access better prices and shared resources
