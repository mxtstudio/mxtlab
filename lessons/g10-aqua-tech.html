Technology in Aquaculture & Capture Fishery

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Fish Harvest Technology Fish Processing Technology Advantages & Disadvantages of Technology

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how technology is used in fish farming, harvesting, and preservation in different environments
  • Describe the steps involved in processing fish products for commercial purposes
  • Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of using technology in capture fishery

Fish Harvest Technology

Fish harvest technology provides employment and economic benefits to large sections of societies. It encompasses various processes of catching aquatic organisms, from the simplest gathering of organisms to the most highly sophisticated systems used to capture large tuna and whales.

The most significant technological developments supporting the evolution of fish harvest technology are:

  • Developments in craft technology — mechanisation of propulsion, gear, and catch handling
  • Introduction of synthetic gear materials — nylon and other synthetic fibres that are stronger, lighter, and more durable than natural materials
  • Developments in acoustic fish detection — sonar systems that locate fish schools underwater; satellite-based remote sensing
  • Electronic navigation and position fixing — GPS technology that has positively influenced the safety of fishermen, providing better communication, navigation, and emergency rescue systems (GMDSS)
  • Awareness of responsible fishing — technology developed to ensure sustainability, protect biodiversity, and improve energy efficiency

Fish Processing Technology

When fish are captured or harvested for commercial purposes, they need preprocessing to be delivered to the next part of the marketing chain in a fresh and undamaged condition. There are four main preprocessing steps:

  • Handling the catch — transferring catch from fishing gear to the vessel; sorting and grading; bleeding, gutting, and washing; chilling; and storing until landing. The key rule: kill fish immediately after catching, cut off the head and gills, wash with clean running water, then put fish on ice in insulated boxes. Get fish to the landing area as fast as possible.
  • Removal of scales — wash the fish, cut off the head, then use a sharp knife to scrape scales toward the head. Turn the fish and scale the other side.
  • Cutting (gutting) — removing the guts (intestines). Gutting should be carried out regardless of the preservation method to be used. Wash thoroughly with clean running water after gutting.
  • Filleting — cutting the flesh into strips parallel to the backbone. Fillets may be with or without skin. Fillets are dipped in brine to enhance appearance, reduce drip, and add a salty flavour. They are then packaged and frozen at -35°C to -40°C and stored at -23°C.

Fish Preservation Technology

Fish preservation methods used in different environments include: control of temperature through refrigeration and freezing; control of water activity through drying, salting, and smoking; physical control of microbial loads through canning; and chemical control through biopreservation and fermentation.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Technology in Capture Fishery

Each fishery technology has advantages and disadvantages that should be balanced in a protective approach. Responsible fishery technology achieves specific management objectives with the minimum damaging side effects.

Advantages

  • More efficient and economically viable fishing operations — e.g., LED lights attached to nets can attract the right catch and guide non-target species to safety
  • Reduction of physical labour required by fishers
  • Improved access to fishery resources
  • Empowers small-scale fishers with market information, allowing better business decisions
  • Improves fisheries management practices and seafood traceability, giving consumers better information

Disadvantages

  • Overfishing — improved technology combined with increased demand and poor management can exhaust fish populations or cause stocks to collapse completely
  • Habitat damage — heavy or large fishing gear can harm the environment. Dredging and bottom trawling impact the sea-floor habitat; in areas with sensitive deep-sea corals, gear can cause long-term damage
  • Cost — technology used in capture fishery is costly, making it inaccessible to many small-scale fishers in developing countries

Key Terms

Fish harvest technologyEquipment and practices used for finding, catching, handling, and distributing fish from capture fisheries or aquaculture.
GPS (Global Positioning System)Satellite-based technology used for navigation and tracking of fishing vessel locations, improving safety and monitoring.
Acoustic fish detectionTechnology that uses sound waves (sonar) to locate schools of fish underwater.
FilletingThe process of cutting the flesh of a fish into strips (fillets) parallel to the backbone, producing a boneless product.
Responsible fishing technologyFishery technology that achieves specific management objectives with minimum damaging side effects on the ecosystem.
LED fishing lightsEnergy-efficient lights attached to fishing nets that can attract target species while guiding non-target species away to safety.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Key fish harvest technologies include acoustic fish detection, GPS navigation, synthetic gear materials, and mechanised propulsion
  • GPS has improved the safety of fishermen through better navigation and emergency rescue systems
  • Four main fish preprocessing steps: handling, scale removal, gutting, and filleting
  • Fish preservation methods include temperature control (refrigeration, freezing), drying, salting, smoking, and canning
  • Technology advantages: efficiency, reduced labour, empowers small-scale fishers, better management data
  • Technology disadvantages: risk of overfishing, habitat damage from trawling, and high cost
