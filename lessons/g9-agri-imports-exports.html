Agricultural Imports & Exports

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
The International Agribusiness Market Agricultural Imports Exporting Agricultural Products

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and discuss the processes and importance of agricultural imports and exports
  • Describe the impact of imports and exports on agribusiness in Papua New Guinea
  • Identify methods that agribusinesses might use to enter international markets

Why International Markets Matter for Agribusiness

There are many reasons for agribusinesses to be interested in international markets. The exporting of agricultural products boosts employment within a country's economy — in production, wholesale and retail trade, services, food processing, and feed processing.

Benefits of entering international markets include:

  • Increased exports and sales revenue
  • Ability to take advantage of scale economies
  • Capture benefits of a global brand
  • Reduce business risk by diversifying across markets
  • Lower costs of production through efficient large-scale operations
  • Access to lower-cost raw materials through international sourcing (imports)
  • Broader access to credit and finance

Technology — particularly internet, email, and mobile communication — has made small agribusinesses in PNG highly competitive with larger firms for emerging-market growth potential. Small agribusiness firms are often more flexible than large corporations, allowing them to adapt quickly to the changing demands of the international food industry.

Two Approaches to Exporting

1. Indirect Exporting

Indirect exporting uses a trading company or export management company to handle the logistics of exporting. These experts manage procedures and regulations, and use their established relationships with buyers and distributors to place the product in international markets. This is considered a low-risk strategy requiring lower investment — suited to first-time exporters and small agribusinesses. The main disadvantage is reduced profitability as the trading company takes a share.

2. Direct Exporting

Direct exporting is where the agribusiness itself handles all the details of exporting — conducting research, establishing contacts in the target country, and setting up its own distribution channels. Firms often open an overseas sales office to manage operations. Direct exporting involves higher investment but offers much greater profit potential and full control over product distribution and brand representation.

Key Terms

ImportGoods and services brought into a country from abroad for sale or use.
ExportGoods and services produced in one country and sold to buyers in another country.
International marketThe global marketplace where goods and services are bought and sold across national borders.
Indirect exportingUsing a trading company or export management company to handle the logistics of exporting on behalf of an agribusiness.
Direct exportingWhere an agribusiness itself handles all the details of exporting — including research, distribution, and overseas contacts.
Scale economiesCost advantages that a business gains by increasing its output — producing more reduces the average cost per unit.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • International trade in agriculture creates employment in production, processing, wholesale, retail, and services
  • Key benefits: higher sales, scale economies, risk diversification, global brand building, and access to credit
  • Indirect exporting uses a trading company to handle logistics — low risk and lower investment
  • Direct exporting is handled by the agribusiness itself — higher investment but greater profit potential and control
  • Technology has made it easier for small PNG agribusinesses to compete in international markets
  • Flexibility and adaptability are key advantages that small agribusiness firms have in global markets
