Poultry Breeds, Management & Technology

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Breeds of Farmed Birds Poultry Production Systems Technology in Poultry Farming

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and categorize breeds of domesticated and farmed birds according to their characteristics, functions, and purposes
  • Compare and contrast the four poultry production systems used in different environments
  • Evaluate the impact of technology used in poultry farming on environments and people

Breeds of Farmed Birds

Birds or chickens can be identified or classified through designated class, breed, variety, and strain.

  • Class — designates a group of breeds developed in a particular geographical area. Examples: American, Asiatic, Mediterranean, English.
  • Breed — a group of birds each possessing a given set of physical features (body shape, skin colour, feathered or non-feathered shanks) that are passed on to offspring.
  • Variety — a subdivision of a breed. Differentiated by plumage colour, comb type, and presence of beard and muffs.
  • Strain or line — families or breeding populations with common traits. Developed for egg production, meat production, or egg colour.

Examples of farmed bird breeds and their purposes:

  • Jersey Giant (American class) — very heavy meat type. Largest breed in the American class. Also a fairly good layer.
  • New Hampshire Red (American class) — dual purpose, selected more for meat than eggs. Fast growing, relatively calm.
  • Langshan (Asiatic class) — general purpose for meat and eggs. Originated in China. Active and quick.
  • Australorp (English class) — generally a very good egg producer with a fairly meaty body. Popular backyard flock bird.
  • Leghorn (Mediterranean class) — egg-type chicken. Small, spritely bird noted for egg production. Good forager.
  • Norfolk Black — good for both meat and eggs. Medium sized; stags 7–8 kg, hens 4–5 kg.

In PNG, both Zenag and Table Birds are major poultry farming companies producing broilers and eggs for local markets.

Poultry Production Systems

Four poultry production systems can be distinguished, particularly in developing countries:

  • 1. Free-range (unimproved backyard) system — scavenging birds with no regular water or feed, little or poor night shelter. Very low inputs. Common in rural PNG villages.
  • 2. Improved backyard system — regular water, supplementary feeding, improved shelter, care of chicks in the first weeks, vaccination against Newcastle disease and other diseases (fowl pox, Gumboro disease, coccidiosis) when necessary.
  • 3. Semi-intensive system — as per the improved backyard system, but with genetically improved breeds and balanced diets. Higher production levels.
  • 4. Small-scale intensive system — further improvements in overall husbandry conditions. Confined housing, controlled feeding, disease prevention programme, and detailed record keeping.

The choice of system is largely determined by the availability of resources and inputs (housing, cages, feed, drugs, time, and attention) and the socio-economic circumstances of the farmer.

Technology in Poultry Farming

Farm technology includes all methods, equipment, and machinery used to improve farming enterprises. Examples of technologies and innovations used in poultry farming in PNG:

  • Hatcheries and incubators — PNG's two large poultry companies (Zenag and Table Birds) use incubators and hatcheries to produce day-old chicks at scale.
  • Battery cages — used for layers to make it efficient to collect eggs and manage large numbers of birds in confined spaces.
  • Automated feeders and waterers — self-refilling when low on feed or water, reducing labour requirements.
  • Lighting systems (brooders) — automatic switches that control light and warmth depending on temperature and humidity.
  • Thermal sensitive lights — lights that activate when an intruder approaches, serving as a security measure.
  • Locally produced feeds — feeds produced using locally grown crops like sweet potato (kaukau) and tapioca to reduce costs.

Processing Line Technology (Zenag and Table Birds)

The full processing chain uses: transport, electrical weighing machines, mechanical slaughter blades, hot baths for feather loosening, plucking machines, cutting and gutting equipment, chillers for storage, packaging machines, and refrigerated trucks for distribution.

Impact of Technology

Technology in poultry farming has positive and negative impacts. Natural environment impacts include air pollution from generator emissions and water pollution from chemical waste disposal. Socio-economic impacts include reduced need for labour (when automation replaces manual work), which can cause unemployment and social problems in communities that depended on farm work for income.

Key Terms

Class (poultry)A group of poultry breeds developed in a particular geographical area — e.g. American, Asiatic, Mediterranean, English classes.
Breed (poultry)A group of birds that each possess a given set of physical features (body shape, skin colour, feathering) that are passed on to offspring.
VarietyA subdivision of a breed, differentiated by characteristics such as plumage colour, comb type, or presence of beard and muffs.
BroilerA chicken bred specifically for meat production, growing rapidly to market weight.
LayerA chicken bred specifically for egg production.
IncubatorA machine that maintains the temperature and humidity required to hatch fertilised eggs, replacing the need for a broody hen.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Poultry breeds are classified by class (geographic origin), breed (physical traits), variety (plumage/comb), and strain (production purpose)
  • Four production systems: free-range, improved backyard, semi-intensive, and small-scale intensive — in order of increasing inputs
  • The choice of system depends on available resources, inputs, and the farmer's socio-economic situation
  • PNG poultry technology includes incubators, battery cages, automated feeders, brooders, and locally produced feeds
  • Zenag and Table Birds use full mechanised processing lines from transport through to packaged retail products
  • Technology impacts: positive (efficiency, lower cost) and negative (pollution, reduced labour, unemployment)
