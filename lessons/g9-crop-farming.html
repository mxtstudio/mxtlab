🚜 Strand 1: Crops · Unit 3

Crop Farming Practices & Management

Grade 9 Papua New Guinea

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify and describe types of crop farming practices used in different contexts
  • Analyse different crop farming practices in different environments
  • Propose ways of improving crop farming practices

Types of Farming Systems

Crop farmers work the land to produce vegetables, fruits, grains, and other crops. Different farming systems are used around the world and in Papua New Guinea:

  • Subsistence farming — farmers grow food mainly for their own family. Characterised by small, scattered land areas, few resources, and minimal technology. Very common in Papua New Guinea.
  • Commercial farming — farming for profit, where food is produced using advanced technology and sold in the market. Often few workers are employed due to mechanisation.
  • Mixed farming — involves both growing crops and raising livestock on the same farm. This spreads risk and improves soil through animal manure.
  • Arable farming — focuses only on growing crops (no livestock) in warm climates.
  • Pastoral farming — focuses on livestock production — dairy farming, beef cattle, or sheep — rather than crops.
  • Intensive farming — uses high levels of inputs (fertiliser, water, labour) per unit of land to maximise yield. Produces more food per hectare but has higher environmental impact.
  • Extensive farming — uses large areas of land with low inputs and low yields per hectare. Common in areas where land is plentiful.
  • Nomadic farming — livestock are herded from place to place to find fresh pasture. Practised in grassland and savannah regions.
  • Shifting cultivation — a traditional practice where land is cleared, farmed for a few years, then abandoned to recover. Still practised in some parts of Papua New Guinea.

Crop Management Practices

Good crop management involves making the right decisions at each stage of production. Key practices include:

  • Planting date — choosing the right time to plant based on season, rainfall, and temperature
  • Plant population — planting the right density so crops are not too crowded (competing) or too sparse (wasting land)
  • Irrigation — supplying water during dry periods to ensure crops get enough moisture
  • Fertilising — adding nutrients (organic or synthetic) to maintain soil fertility
  • Weeding — removing competing plants that reduce yield
  • Pest and disease control — managing insects, fungi, and other organisms that damage crops
  • Crop rotation — alternating different crops on the same land each season to maintain soil nutrients and break pest cycles

Improving Crop Farming Practices

There are many ways to improve crop farming to increase productivity:

  1. Multiple cropping — growing more than one crop on the same piece of land during the year. For example, growing sweet potato after harvesting maize.
  2. Improved inputs — using quality seeds, fertilisers, and pesticides to improve yield
  3. Better irrigation — building or improving water supply systems
  4. Agricultural education — training farmers in modern techniques and best practices
  5. Better transport and markets — connecting farmers to markets so they can sell produce and earn enough to invest back into their farms

Sustainable Farming Practices

Sustainable agriculture meets society's present food needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs. Key sustainable practices include:

  • Permaculture — designing food production systems that mimic natural ecosystems
  • Crop rotation and polycultures — growing multiple crops together to maintain soil health
  • Using renewable energy — solar pumps for irrigation instead of diesel
  • Agroforestry — planting trees among crops to improve soil, shade, and biodiversity
⚠️ Poor agricultural practices — such as overuse of chemicals — reduce soil porosity, increase salinity and alkalinity, and disturb the natural nutrient composition of soil. These effects reduce productivity over time.

📖 Key Terms

Subsistence farmingFarming where food is grown mainly for the farmer\'s own family with little surplus for sale
Crop rotationAlternating different crops on the same land each season to maintain soil fertility and control pests
IrrigationThe artificial supply of water to crops when natural rainfall is insufficient
Multiple croppingGrowing more than one crop on the same piece of land during the same year
PermacultureA food production system designed to mimic how plants grow naturally in ecosystems
Shifting cultivationA traditional farming practice where land is cleared, farmed for a few years, then left to recover

📌 Summary

  • Farming systems include subsistence, commercial, mixed, arable, pastoral, intensive, and extensive
  • Shifting cultivation and subsistence farming are common traditional practices in Papua New Guinea
  • Good crop management involves correct planting time, watering, fertilising, weeding, and pest control
  • Multiple cropping — growing more than one crop per year — increases productivity
  • Sustainable farming protects soil and ensures land can continue to produce for future generations
