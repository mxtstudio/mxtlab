Poultry Farming Principles & Systems

📖 4 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Free-Range Systems Semi-Intensive Systems Intensive Systems Management Practices

Learning Objectives

  • Describe different poultry production systems and their characteristics
  • Explain management practices for successful poultry farming
  • Compare advantages and disadvantages of each system

Free-Range (Extensive) Systems

Birds have unlimited access to outdoor areas and grazing land.

Characteristics

  • Birds roam freely to forage for food
  • Minimal enclosed housing needed
  • Low capital investment
  • Low feed costs (birds find insects, worms, plants)
  • Lower productivity and egg/meat output
  • Higher risk of predation and disease

Best for

  • Small-scale village farming
  • Subsistence poultry keeping
  • Areas with abundant land and good climate
  • Producing premium "free-range" products

Semi-Intensive Systems

Birds have both enclosed houses and outdoor pens or runs.

Characteristics

  • Birds housed at night for safety and protection
  • Outdoor runs (pens) allow some grazing and foraging
  • Supplementary feed provided in addition to foraged food
  • Moderate capital investment for simple housing
  • Medium productivity and lower disease risk than free-range
  • Better control over health and nutrition than free-range

Best for

  • Small to medium-scale farming
  • Balancing productivity with lower input costs
  • Areas with predation risk
  • Farmers wanting good balance of profit and sustainability

Intensive Systems

Birds are confined in buildings with full environmental control.

Battery Cage System (for eggs)

  • Birds housed in small cages (multiple birds per cage)
  • Automated feeding, watering, egg collection
  • Very high productivity (300+ eggs per bird per year)
  • Concentrated waste management challenges
  • Controversial due to animal welfare concerns

Deep Litter System (for meat and eggs)

  • Birds on bedding material (wood shavings, straw) on floor
  • Easier to manage than cages; better welfare
  • Requires larger buildings and more labour
  • Bedding accumulates litter — ammonia management important

Controlled Environment Features

  • Temperature control — heating in cold, ventilation in heat
  • Lighting control — 14-16 hours daily for layers
  • Automatic feeding and watering — ensures consistent supply
  • Biosecurity — controlled access prevents disease entry
  • Waste management — manure collected regularly

Best for

  • Commercial poultry production
  • Areas with high land costs
  • Climate extremes (too hot or cold)
  • Maximum productivity and profitability

Management Practices Across Systems

Housing & Environment

  • Adequate space: 1-2 birds per m² for layers; 3-4 birds per m² for broilers
  • Proper ventilation to remove ammonia and moisture
  • Temperature: 18-24°C optimal; broilers slightly warmer
  • Light: 14-16 hours daily for layers; 23-24 hours for broilers
  • Roosting bars and nesting boxes for comfort and laying

Feeding & Nutrition

  • Starter feed (0-4 weeks) — high protein (20-24%) for growth
  • Grower feed (4-16 weeks) — medium protein (16-18%) for continued growth
  • Layer feed (for hens) — balanced with calcium for egg shell quality
  • Finisher feed (broilers only) — lower fibre for rapid weight gain
  • Fresh water always available

Health & Biosecurity

  • Vaccination against common diseases (Newcastle, Gumboro)
  • Regular parasite control (worms, mites, lice)
  • Quarantine new birds before adding to flock
  • Strict hygiene: clean feeders, waterers, and housing
  • Isolation of sick or injured birds
  • Regular bird counts to detect missing birds or mortality

Record Keeping

  • Daily mortality records
  • Feed consumption — indicates health problems if drops
  • Egg production rates and quality
  • Health treatments and vaccinations
  • Income and expenses for profitability analysis

Key Terms

Free-rangeA system where poultry have unlimited outdoor access to forage for food
Semi-intensiveA system combining indoor housing with outdoor pens or runs
IntensiveA confined system with complete environmental control — highest productivity
BiosecurityMeasures to prevent disease entry and spread through hygiene and controlled access
BroodingProviding warmth and care for young birds — using heat lamps or broody hens

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Free-range systems have low costs but lower productivity; best for subsistence farming
  • Semi-intensive systems balance productivity with sustainability; suitable for small-scale commercial farming
  • Intensive systems maximize output but require high investment and careful management
  • Proper housing provides space, ventilation, temperature control, and lighting for bird welfare
  • Nutrition, health management, and record-keeping are essential for successful poultry farming
