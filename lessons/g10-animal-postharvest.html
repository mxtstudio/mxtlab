Post-Harvest Processing of Animal Products

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Food Processing Methods Food Preservation Techniques Benefits & Drawbacks of Processing

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the objectives and methods of food processing for animal products
  • Describe food preservation techniques used to extend the shelf life of animal products
  • Evaluate the benefits and drawbacks of food processing for animal product quality

What is Food Processing?

Food processing is the process of transforming food items into a form that can be used. It covers the processing of raw materials into food via different physical and chemical processes. Various activities include mincing, cooking, canning, liquefaction, pickling, macerating, and emulsification.

Processing takes clean harvested crops, or butchered and slaughtered animal products, and produces attractive, marketable, and in several cases, long-lasting food products. However, food processing can also lower the nutritional value of food and might include additives that affect health.

Objectives of Food Processing

  • Boosts the shelf life of food products
  • Prevents food contamination
  • Enables food storage and transportation
  • Turns raw food materials into attractive, marketable products
  • Provides employment to a large population

Food Processing Methods for Animal Products

The following methods are applied for the proper processing of food:

  • Peeling off outer layers of raw materials
  • Chopping or slicing
  • Mincing
  • Fermentation
  • Emulsification
  • Cooking
  • Mixing
  • Pasteurisation (boiling milk to kill harmful microorganisms)
  • Packaging
  • Spray drying

Food Preservation Techniques

Food preservation prevents the growth of fungi, bacteria, and microorganisms, and slows down oxidation of fats that would cause rancidity.

  • Drying — exposure to sunlight or hot air to evaporate moisture from food, preventing microorganisms from growing. One of the oldest and most traditional techniques used throughout Papua New Guinea.
  • Cooling and refrigeration — slows down the growth of microorganisms and enzyme activity responsible for rotting. Meat, dairy products, and fish are stored in a refrigerator, increasing their shelf life.
  • Freezing — one of the most common processes used domestically and commercially to preserve a wide range of foods.
  • Heating — most microorganisms and spores can be destroyed by applying sufficient heat. Example: boiling of milk (pasteurisation).
  • Pickling — preserving food in an edible antimicrobial liquid. Two types: fermentation pickling (bacteria in liquid produce organic preservation agents) and chemical pickling (food preserved in a liquid that destroys microorganisms).

Benefits and Drawbacks of Food Processing

Benefits

  • Reduces the number of harmful bacteria in food that can cause diseases (drying and pickling dehydrate food and alter pH, preventing harmful microorganism growth)
  • Improves the shelf life of food products
  • Reduces health inequalities and major health concerns by making safe food more accessible

Drawbacks

  • Processed food may contain artificial ingredients
  • Processing foods to make them pleasant to eat can lead to overconsumption
  • Processed foods are a major source of added sugar, which is associated with health risks
  • Some processing reduces the nutritional value of food

Key Terms

Food processingThe process of transforming raw food items into a form that can be used or marketed — through physical and chemical processes such as mincing, cooking, canning, and packaging.
PasteurisationA heat treatment process used to kill harmful microorganisms in food products, especially milk. Named after Louis Pasteur.
DryingA preservation method that removes moisture from food using sunlight or hot air, preventing microorganisms from growing.
Cooling and refrigerationA preservation method that slows down microbial growth and enzyme activity to extend the shelf life of meat, dairy, and fish.
PicklingA preservation method using an edible antimicrobial liquid (brine or acid). Can be fermentation-based or chemical.
Shelf lifeThe length of time a food product remains safe and suitable for consumption under recommended storage conditions.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Food processing transforms raw animal products into marketable food through physical and chemical processes
  • Objectives of food processing: extend shelf life, prevent contamination, enable transport, create marketable products
  • Drying is one of the oldest preservation techniques — it removes moisture to prevent microbial growth
  • Refrigeration slows microorganism growth; freezing stops it — both extend the shelf life of meat, fish, and dairy
  • Pickling preserves food in an antimicrobial liquid — either through fermentation or chemical means
  • Benefits of processing include extended shelf life and food safety; drawbacks include reduced nutritional value
