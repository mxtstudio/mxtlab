Plant Propagation Technology

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Sexual Propagation Asexual / Vegetative Propagation Technology in Plant Propagation

Learning Objectives

  • Explain what plant propagation is and distinguish between sexual and asexual propagation
  • Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of different propagation methods
  • Identify how technology has improved plant propagation in horticulture and crop farming

Sexual Propagation (Seed Propagation)

Sexual propagation involves the union of pollen and egg, leading to seed formation. It is an old, easy, simple, and effective technique for ornamental plants, vegetables, fruits, and medicinal plants.

Advantages: produces large numbers quickly; seeds can be handled in large numbers; can produce hybrids with desirable traits.

Disadvantages: some plants produce no viable seeds; some seeds are very difficult or slow to germinate; causes genetic variability — offspring may not be identical to the parent plant.

Asexual Propagation (Vegetative Propagation)

Asexual propagation uses vegetative parts of plants — leaves, stems, roots, or modified organs — to produce new plants. It produces clones: plants genetically identical to the parent. Methods include cutting, division, layering, grafting, budding, and tissue culture.

Advantages: all offspring are true-to-type; suitable for plants that cannot be propagated from seeds; decreases time to flowering (especially grafting and budding).

Disadvantages: only a small number of plants can be produced from each parent (except tissue culture); requires significant labour.

Tissue Culture

Tissue culture grows new plants from small pieces of plant tissue in a controlled laboratory environment.

Advantages: large numbers produced in a short time from a tiny amount of tissue; plants are more likely to be disease-free; can be done year-round regardless of season.

Disadvantages: requires more labour and higher cost; plants may be less resilient if not properly acclimatised; material must be carefully screened before culture.

Technology in Plant Propagation

Technology has improved plant propagation in several important ways:

  • Vegetative crop solutions — used in fields, greenhouses, and laboratories to produce cuttings or transplants at scale.
  • Heat propagators — devices that maintain warmth and moisture in newly propagated plants, significantly improving survival rates.
  • Seed propagation mats — electric heated rubber mats that maintain consistent soil temperature for seedlings, allowing year-round gardening even in cold conditions.
  • Tissue culture expansion — most commonly applied to herbaceous ornamentals and fruits. Provides uniform, disease-free plants where virus infestations had previously been a serious problem.

Key Terms

Plant propagationThe process of growing new plants from a variety of sources: seeds, cuttings, and other plant parts.
Sexual propagationPropagation involving the union of pollen and egg, leading to seed formation. Also called seed propagation.
Asexual propagationPropagation using vegetative parts of the plant (leaves, stems, roots) to produce plants identical to the parent.
Tissue cultureA propagation technique that grows new plants from small pieces of plant tissue in a laboratory environment.
CloneA group of plants derived from the same parent plant by asexual propagation — all genetically identical to the parent.
Heat propagatorA device used in horticulture to maintain warmth and moisture in newly propagated plants, improving survival rate.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • Plant propagation grows new plants from seeds (sexual) or vegetative parts (asexual)
  • Sexual propagation produces large numbers quickly but offspring may vary genetically
  • Asexual propagation produces clones identical to the parent — best for maintaining desirable traits
  • Tissue culture produces disease-free, uniform plants year-round from tiny amounts of plant tissue
  • Heat propagators and seed mats use technology to maintain ideal conditions for plant establishment
  • The main drive behind propagation technology is increasing food production to meet growing demand
