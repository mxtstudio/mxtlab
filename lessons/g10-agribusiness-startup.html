🚀 Strand 5: Agribusiness · Unit 3 · Grade 10

Starting Up an Agribusiness

📖 3 lessons 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea Curriculum
Managing Risk in Agribusiness Sources of Start-up Capital Managing Start-up Capital

Learning Objectives

  • Analyse and discuss the best practices of risk management for agribusiness
  • Identify and explain the processes of obtaining start-up capital for an agribusiness
  • Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of different sources of finance for agribusiness start-up

Sources of Risk in Agribusiness

The most common sources of risk in farming can be divided into five areas:

  • Production risk — Crop and livestock performance depends on biological processes affected by weather and pests. Low rainfall, drought, hail, or heavy rains could damage or wipe out crops. Pest or disease outbreaks can cause major losses.
  • Marketing risk — Changes in prices are beyond the control of any individual farmer. Supply and demand changes unexpectedly. When farmers commit resources to production, they do not know for certain what prices they will receive.
  • Financial risk — Occurs when money is borrowed to finance the farm business. Smallholder farmers who borrow at high interest rates may have difficulty with debt repayment when prices are low or yields are poor.
  • Institutional risk — Unpredictable changes in services from banks, cooperatives, marketing organisations, and government extension services. Government policy changes (subsidies, food quality regulations) can have a major impact on farm business performance.
  • Human and personal risk — Risks caused by illness, death, accidents, and labour migration. In PNG, migration away from rural areas can cause serious labour shortages for the farm.

Risk Management Strategies

  • Diversification — growing multiple crops or combining crops and livestock to spread risk.
  • Crop insurance — financial products that compensate farmers for losses caused by weather events, pests, or disease.
  • Contract farming — agreeing a fixed price with a buyer before planting, reducing marketing risk.
  • Building financial reserves — maintaining savings to absorb losses in bad seasons.
  • Joining cooperatives — pooling resources and market access to reduce costs and improve bargaining power.

Obtaining and Managing Start-up Capital

Sources of start-up capital for an agribusiness include:

  • Personal savings — the most common source for small-scale farmers in PNG. No debt or interest involved, but limited in scale.
  • Family and community contributions — informal funding from family members or community groups. Common in PNG wantok networks.
  • Bank loans — formal credit from commercial banks. Requires collateral and a business plan. Interest payments are a financial risk if revenue is low.
  • Government grants and programmes — the PNG government and organisations such as the Small Business Development Corporation (SBDC) offer grants and subsidised loans.
  • Microfinance — small loans from microfinance institutions suited to smallholder farmers without traditional collateral.
  • NGO and development funding — non-government organisations sometimes provide start-up support for community agribusiness projects.

Managing start-up capital wisely requires keeping detailed records of all expenditure, prioritising essential inputs, avoiding unnecessary debt, and planning cash flow to ensure the business can meet its obligations while waiting for the first harvest revenue.

Key Terms

Production riskRisk arising from the dependence of crop and livestock performance on weather, pests, and disease — all beyond the farmer's control.
Marketing riskRisk arising from unpredictable changes in prices and costs of farm products.
Financial riskRisk arising when money is borrowed to finance the farm business — including uncertainty about future interest rates and ability to repay.
Institutional riskRisk from unpredictable changes in the services provided by banks, cooperatives, and government extension services.
Human riskRisk to the farm business caused by illness, death, accidents, or labour migration away from the farm.
Start-up capitalThe initial finance needed to establish and begin operating a new business.

📝 Summary — What You Should Know

  • The five sources of risk in agribusiness are: production, marketing, financial, institutional, and human risk
  • Production risk arises from weather, pests, and disease affecting crop and livestock performance
  • Marketing risk comes from unpredictable changes in product prices beyond the farmer's control
  • Financial risk occurs when borrowed money cannot be repaid due to low yields or low prices
  • Risk management strategies include diversification, insurance, contract farming, and joining cooperatives
  • Sources of start-up capital include personal savings, family contributions, bank loans, government grants, and microfinance
