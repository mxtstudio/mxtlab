Types of Poultry & Their Behaviour

Grade 9 Strand 2: Animals · Unit 4
Benchmark

9.2.4.1 Investigate and explain the importance of Poultry production.

Topic

Poultry

Types of Domesticated Poultry

Poultry refers to domesticated birds raised for meat, eggs, or feathers. Main types include:

Chickens (Fowl)

  • Most commonly raised poultry worldwide
  • Males are roosters; females are hens
  • Can be raised for eggs, meat, or both
  • Relatively easy to manage and fast to mature (6-8 weeks for meat)
  • Excellent feed conversion (1-2 kg feed per 1 kg meat)

Ducks

  • Water birds requiring access to water for health and wellbeing
  • Lay blue or brown eggs
  • Meat is fattier and richer in flavour than chicken
  • Hardier than chickens; can convert poor quality feeds
  • Excellent for small-scale farming and marshy areas

Geese

  • Larger than ducks; require more space
  • Excellent at converting grass to meat
  • Good mothers; broody and protective
  • Long lifespan (15-20 years); reproductive peak at 2-3 years
  • Produce high-quality meat and down feathers

Turkeys

  • Large, meat-type birds
  • Longer growing period (16-20 weeks) than chickens
  • Popular in Western countries for special occasions
  • Less common in Papua New Guinea due to management demands

Guinea fowls

  • Small birds raised for meat
  • Better disease resistance than chickens
  • Hardy and good foragers
  • Meat is considered a delicacy in some cultures

Poultry Breeds & Characteristics

Meat Breeds (Broilers)

  • Fast-growing and heavy (2-2.5 kg at 6-8 weeks)
  • Examples: Cornish Cross, Cobb, Ross strains
  • Fed specialized commercial diets
  • Large breasts and meat yields

Egg Breeds (Layers)

  • Light body weight but high egg production (300+ eggs/year)
  • Examples: Leghorn, Rhode Island Red, Sussex
  • Lower feed intake than broilers
  • Productive for 2-3 years before declining

Dual-Purpose Breeds

  • Good for both meat and eggs
  • Examples: Australorp, Brahma, Wyandotte
  • Suited to smallholder farmers with mixed production goals

Indigenous/Local Breeds

  • Adapted to local environments
  • More disease-resistant and hardy
  • Slower growth but tasty meat
  • Good foragers; thrive with minimal external inputs

Poultry Behaviour & Welfare

Natural Behaviours

  • Scratching — birds search for food and maintain feather condition
  • Dust bathing — maintains feather health and controls parasites
  • Roosting — perching at height for safety during sleep
  • Nesting — hens need quiet, private spaces to lay eggs
  • Social hierarchy — pecking order determines dominance

Welfare Requirements

  • Space — minimum 0.3 m² per bird in cages; 1-1.5 m² in pens
  • Perches and roosting bars — satisfy perching behaviour
  • Nesting boxes — one per 4 birds for layers
  • Litter for scratching — straw, wood shavings, or sand
  • Light — 14-16 hours daily for optimal egg production
  • Ventilation — removes ammonia and moisture

Selection Based on Purpose

For Egg Production

  • Choose egg breeds: Leghorns, Rhode Island Reds
  • Select females (hens) only
  • Expect 250-300 eggs per bird per year
  • Productive for 2-3 years

For Meat Production

  • Choose meat breeds: Cornish Cross, Cobb, Ross
  • Rapid growth to 2-2.5 kg in 6-8 weeks
  • High feed conversion efficiency
  • Market-ready quickly

For Small-scale/Village Farming

  • Dual-purpose breeds: Australorp, Wyandotte
  • Or indigenous local breeds
  • Lower input requirements
  • Good foragers; thrive on kitchen scraps and insects

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Define poultry and explain its importance in PNG.
  • Differentiate between domesticated and farmed birds.
  • Examine and evaluate the characteristics of domesticated and farmed birds.
Teacher Notes

Teaching Suggestion: Show different types of poultry birds common in PNG. Discuss the difference between village chickens and commercial layers/broilers.

Essential Questions to ask students:
  1. What is poultry and why is it important?
  2. What are the different types of birds used in farming?
  3. How are domesticated and farmed birds different?

Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which bird is best adapted to wetland areas?

Q2: How fast do broiler chickens grow to market weight?

Q3: What is the pecking order?

Q4: What space is needed per bird in intensive housing?

Q5: Which breed is best for small-scale village farming?
