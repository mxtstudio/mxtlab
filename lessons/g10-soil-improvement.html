Soil Improvement Practices

Grade 10 Strand 1: Crops · Unit 1

Why Soil Improvement Matters

In Grade 9, we learned how soil forms and what it contains. In Grade 10, we focus on how to improve soil that has become degraded, compacted, or depleted of nutrients — and how to sustain its fertility over time.

Soils become depleted when: crops remove nutrients faster than they are replaced; erosion removes topsoil; overuse of chemicals destroys micro-organisms; or deforestation exposes soil to sun and rain.

Natural Soil Improvement Techniques

Natural methods improve soil without chemical inputs. They work with ecological processes:

  • Adding organic matter (compost) — decomposed plant and animal material improves soil structure, water retention, and nutrient content. Compost is made from kitchen scraps, crop residues, and animal manure
  • Green manure / cover crops — growing legumes (beans, peanuts, mucuna) and then ploughing them back into the soil before flowering. Legumes fix nitrogen from the air into the soil
  • Mulching — covering the soil surface with organic material (straw, leaves, grass) to reduce moisture loss, control weeds, and gradually add organic matter as it decomposes
  • Crop rotation — alternating crops each season to prevent depletion of specific nutrients and break pest cycles
  • Agroforestry — planting trees alongside crops to add organic matter through leaf litter, fix nitrogen, and prevent erosion
  • Intercropping — growing two or more crops together. A nitrogen-fixing legume grown with a cereal crop benefits both
  • Worm farming (vermicomposting) — using earthworms to break down organic waste into rich compost

Artificial Soil Improvement Techniques

Artificial methods use manufactured inputs to quickly improve specific soil properties:

  • Synthetic fertilisers (NPK) — manufactured chemicals that supply nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) directly to plants. Fast-acting but can damage soil biology if overused
  • Liming — adding agricultural lime (calcium carbonate) to raise the pH of acidic soils, making nutrients more available to plants
  • Gypsum — adding calcium sulfate to improve clay soil structure and reduce sodicity (excess sodium)
  • Irrigation — supplying water artificially to prevent moisture stress that limits nutrient uptake
  • Soil conditioners — manufactured products (e.g. zeolite, biochar) that improve soil water retention and nutrient holding capacity
Balance: The best soil management combines natural and artificial techniques. Synthetic fertilisers can boost yields quickly, but relying on them alone degrades soil health over time. Natural methods build long-term fertility.

Strategies for Sustaining Soil Fertility

Sustaining soil fertility means maintaining its ability to support crop production indefinitely. Key strategies:

  • Return crop residues and organic waste to the soil rather than burning them
  • Avoid leaving soil bare — always maintain ground cover to prevent erosion
  • Test soil regularly to know exactly what nutrients are needed
  • Apply fertiliser at the right time, in the right amount, and in the right place
  • Reduce tillage (ploughing) to protect soil structure and micro-organism communities
  • Maintain windbreaks and vegetation on slopes to prevent erosion
