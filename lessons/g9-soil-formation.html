Soil — Types & Formation

📖 5 sections ⏱ ~12 min read 🧪 Quiz included
🎯 Benchmark

9.1.1.1 Identify different types of soil and explain how they are formed.

📌 Topic

Soil Formation

What is Soil?

Soil is the thin layer of material covering the earth's surface that supports plant life. All soils are different because of where and how they formed. Basically, soil is formed by the weathering or breakdown of rock on the upper layers of the solid earth — and this very rock is called the parent material.

Did You Know? A single teaspoon of healthy soil contains more living organisms than there are people on Earth. These micro-organisms are essential for breaking down organic matter and releasing nutrients.

The Five Factors That Form Soil

Five factors interact together to determine what type of soil forms in any location. Remember them using CLORPT — Climate, Living organisms, Original material (parent), Relief (topography), Parent material, Time.

1

🌱 Living Organisms

Bacteria, fungi, earthworms, and plant roots all contribute organic material and speed up breakdown of rock into soil particles.

2

🌧 Climate

Temperature and rainfall determine how fast weathering occurs and what types of minerals form. PNG's high rainfall accelerates soil formation.

3

🪨 Parent Material

The type of rock being broken down determines the mineral content of the soil. Volcanic soils in PNG are typically very fertile.

4

⏳ Time

The longer rock weathers, the more developed the soil becomes. Deep, mature soils take thousands of years to form.

5

⛰ Topography

The slope and position of land affects drainage and erosion. Steep slopes lose topsoil quickly while flat areas accumulate it.

🇵🇬
PNG Connection Papua New Guinea's volcanic highlands produce some of the most fertile soils in the Pacific. The combination of volcanic parent material, high rainfall, and warm temperatures creates ideal conditions for agriculture.

How Weathering Breaks Down Rock

Weathering is the process by which rock is broken down into the particles that eventually become soil. There are three types — physical, chemical, and biological.

Physical Weathering

  • Moving water rubs stones together, breaking off small particles
  • Heat expands rock; cold contracts it — repeated cycles crack it apart
  • Wind blows sand against rock, slowly wearing it down

Chemical Weathering

  • CO₂ in air forms weak carbonic acid that dissolves limestone
  • Rainwater reacts with minerals, changing their composition
  • Oxidation turns iron-rich rocks red or orange
✅
Key Point Plants and animals also contribute to soil formation — when they die and decompose, they add the organic matter that makes soil dark, fertile, and alive.

Physical Properties of Soil

Physical properties are aspects of the soil that can be seen and touched. Understanding them helps farmers choose the right crops and farming methods.

TextureProportion of sand, silt, and clay particlesDetermines water retention and drainage

ColourDark = high organic matter; Red = iron oxidesIndicates fertility and drainage quality

"Loam — a balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay — is considered the ideal soil texture for most crops because it holds moisture yet drains well."

— Soil Science Principles

Chemical Properties of Soil

The chemical properties of soil affect its ability to supply nutrients to plants and the activity of micro-organisms living in the soil.

⚠️
Watch Out — Lateritic Soils Soils that lose too many nutrients can become lateritic and barren. Lateritic soils contain so much aluminium and iron that they are toxic to plants — even weeds struggle to grow in them. This is a serious problem in parts of PNG where forests have been cleared.
📚

Key Terms

Parent materialThe original rock that breaks down through weathering to form soil.
WeatheringThe physical, chemical, or biological breakdown of rock into smaller particles.
Aggregate (ped)A clump of soil particles cemented together; gives soil its structure.
Soil pHA measure of how acidic or alkaline soil is, on a scale from 0–14.
Lateritic soilNutrient-depleted soil dominated by aluminium and iron; toxic to most plants.
PorosityThe proportion of pore spaces in soil; determines water and air holding capacity.

✅ Lesson Summary

  • Soil is made of mineral matter (45%), water (25%), air (25%), and organic matter (5%)
  • Five factors form soil: living organisms, climate, parent material, time, and topography
  • Weathering breaks rock down physically (water, heat/cold, wind) and chemically (acids, oxidation)
  • Physical properties include texture, structure, colour, and porosity
  • Soil pH should be 6–7 for most crops; lateritic soils are a serious problem in PNG

💡 Quick Fact

It takes approximately 500 years to form just 1 inch (2.5 cm) of topsoil naturally. Protecting existing soil is critical for PNG's food security.

🎓

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Describe the different types of soil and how soil is formed.
  • Identify and describe the different components of soil.
  • Explain the five factors that influence soil formation.
👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes

Teaching Suggestion: Use local soil samples to demonstrate differences in texture and composition. Ask students to bring soil from their gardens.

Essential Questions to ask students:
  1. How is soil formed?
  2. What are the components of soil?
  3. What are the different types of soil?
