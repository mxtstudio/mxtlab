🏠 Strand 2: Animals · Unit 3

Animal Farming Practices & Management

Grade 9 Papua New Guinea

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain different animal farming systems and their characteristics
  • Describe proper housing, feeding, and health management for farm animals
  • Analyse the role of breeding and reproduction in animal production

Animal Production Systems

Farm animals can be managed using different systems, each with distinct characteristics:

Extensive Systems

  • Animals graze freely on large pastures with minimal human input
  • Low input costs but low productivity
  • Animals roam and find their own forage
  • Suitable for cattle and sheep in areas with abundant land
  • Common in pastoral communities and rangeland areas

Semi-Intensive Systems

  • Animals kept in enclosures but also allowed to graze
  • Supplementary feeding with grains and concentrates
  • Moderate capital investment required
  • Better control over animal health and nutrition
  • Higher productivity than extensive but lower than intensive

Intensive Systems

  • Animals confined in close housing (pens, cages, sheds)
  • Complete diet provided (no grazing)
  • High input costs (feed, labour, facilities)
  • High productivity and meat/egg/milk output
  • Requires careful management of waste and disease
  • Common for poultry and pig farming

Housing Requirements

Proper housing protects animals from weather, predators, and disease:

  • Adequate space — prevents stress and disease spread
  • Good ventilation — removes ammonia and moisture
  • Clean bedding — improves hygiene and animal comfort
  • Feeding and drinking areas — placed separate from toilets
  • Temperature control — shelter from extreme heat and cold
  • Easy to clean — reduces disease and parasites

Feeding & Nutrition

Animals require balanced diets with proper nutrients:

  • Protein — for growth and muscle development
  • Energy — from carbohydrates and fats
  • Minerals — calcium, phosphorus for bones and milk production
  • Vitamins — for immunity and health
  • Water — essential for all bodily functions

Feed types vary by animal: ruminants eat hay and grass; monogastrics need grains and protein sources.

Health Management

Preventive care reduces disease and improves productivity:

  • Vaccination — prevents infectious diseases
  • Parasite control — regular worming and pest management
  • Hygiene — clean housing and feeding equipment reduce disease transmission
  • Veterinary care — prompt treatment of illness
  • Record keeping — tracks health problems and treatments
  • Isolation of sick animals — prevents disease spread

Breeding & Reproduction

Selective breeding improves animal productivity:

  • Select superior animals — those with desirable traits (growth, milk production, disease resistance)
  • Understand reproductive cycles — time breeding during fertile periods (oestrus)
  • Avoid inbreeding — which reduces genetic diversity and health
  • Record genetics — keep track of parentage and traits
  • Improve herd quality — over generations through selective mating

📖 Key Terms

Extensive systemA farming system where animals graze freely with minimal human input
Intensive systemA farming system where animals are confined and given complete diets — high input and output
OestrusThe fertile period in female animals when they are ready to breed
InbreedingBreeding related animals — can reduce genetic diversity and health
Selective breedingChoosing animals with desirable traits to produce offspring with improved characteristics

📌 Summary

  • Three main farming systems exist: extensive (free grazing), semi-intensive (mixed), and intensive (confined)
  • Proper housing provides shelter, ventilation, space, and clean conditions
  • Animals need balanced nutrition with protein, energy, minerals, and vitamins
  • Preventive health care reduces disease through vaccination, hygiene, and parasite control
  • Selective breeding improves productivity over generations by choosing superior animals
