Strand 5: Agribusiness · Unit 1

Financial Statements & Cash Flow

Grade 11 Agriculture

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and explain the four main types of financial statements
  • Demonstrate how to prepare and analyse a basic cash flow statement
  • Explain the difference between profitability and liquidity in agribusiness

The Four Types of Financial Statements

  1. Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position) — shows what the business owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the owner's equity at a specific point in time.
  2. Income Statement (Profit and Loss Statement) — reports income earned and expenses incurred over a period. Net profit = income minus expenses.
  3. Cash Flow Statement — presents the movement of cash in and out of the business through three activities: operations, investing, and financing.
  4. Statement of Changes in Equity — details the movement in owner's equity over the reporting period.

Cash Flow Statement Analysis

The cash flow statement tracks money moving through the business across three sections:

  • Operating activities — cash from day-to-day business (buying supplies, paying wages, receiving payments).
  • Investing activities — cash used to buy or received from selling long-term assets (equipment, vehicles, land).
  • Financing activities — cash from lenders and investors minus loan repayments and dividend payments.

Key insight: a business can be profitable but cash-poor. A farm may show a profit on its income statement but struggle to pay wages if customers have not paid yet (accounts receivable). The cash flow statement reveals this problem.

Profitability vs Liquidity

  • Profitability measures long-term financial performance — how well the business generates profit. Shown in the income statement.
  • Liquidity measures the ability to meet short-term obligations — whether the business can pay its bills today. Shown in the balance sheet's current assets section.

A company can be highly profitable but illiquid — earning well long-term but unable to pay bills today. Both profitability and liquidity are essential for a healthy agribusiness.

Key Terms

Cash flow statementA financial statement showing all cash inflows and outflows of a business over a period — tracking cash from operations, investment, and financing.
ProfitabilityHow well a business is performing in terms of generating profit relative to its revenue and costs — shown in the income statement.
LiquidityThe ability of a business to meet its short-term financial obligations — having enough cash or near-cash assets to pay bills when due.

Summary

  • The four financial statements: Balance Sheet, Income Statement, Cash Flow Statement, Statement of Changes in Equity
  • The cash flow statement tracks inflows and outflows through operations, investing, and financing activities
  • A business can be profitable but cash-poor — because profit and cash are different things
  • Profitability measures long-term performance; liquidity measures ability to meet short-term obligations
  • Net cash flow is the overall change in a business\
