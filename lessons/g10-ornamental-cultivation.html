Cultivation of Ornamental & Bedding Plants

Grade 10 Strand 1: Crops · Unit 6

Cultivation of Ornamental Plants

The cultivation of ornamental plants is called floriculture. For plants to be considered ornamental, they require specific work and care — including pruning, training, and regular maintenance — to maintain their decorative appearance.

Ornamental plants can be cultivated in a variety of settings:

  • Open ground gardens — planted directly in prepared garden beds with well-drained, nutrient-rich soil.
  • Containers and pots — allows ornamentals to be grown on patios, balconies, or indoors. Gives control over soil type, moisture, and feeding.
  • Greenhouses — controlled environments used for tropical and tender ornamentals that cannot survive outdoors in cooler climates.
  • Nurseries — commercial facilities where ornamentals are propagated and grown before sale to the public or for landscaping projects.

Propagating Ornamental Plants

  • Seed propagation — suitable for many annuals and biennials (e.g., marigold, sunflower, zinnia).
  • Cuttings — a stem, leaf, or root cutting taken from the parent plant and placed in a rooting medium. Used for shrubs, roses, and perennials.
  • Division — clumping plants are dug up and divided into sections, each with roots attached, and replanted.
  • Layering — a stem is bent to the ground, partially buried, and allowed to root before being separated from the parent.
  • Grafting and budding — used for woody ornamentals such as roses and ornamental fruit trees.
  • Tissue culture — used commercially to produce large numbers of uniform, disease-free ornamentals (particularly orchids and ferns).

Care and Maintenance of Ornamental Plants

  • Watering — ornamentals need consistent moisture but must not be overwatered.
  • Fertilising — regular feeding with balanced fertilisers during the growing season promotes healthy growth and flowering.
  • Pruning and deadheading — removing spent flowers (deadheading) encourages continued blooming. Pruning maintains shape and removes diseased material.
  • Pest and disease control — ornamentals are susceptible to aphids, scale insects, fungal diseases, and root rot. Regular inspection and timely treatment prevents spread.
  • Mulching — applying organic mulch conserves soil moisture, suppresses weeds, and moderates soil temperature.
  • Staking and training — tall or climbing ornamentals require stakes, trellises, or frames for support.
